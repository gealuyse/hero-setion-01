# 01 — Strategy Brief
## Personal Brand + Portfolio Positioning

> **Purpose**  
> Use this file for brand strategy, portfolio narrative, homepage messaging, interview positioning, LinkedIn copy, and high-level AI handoff.

---

## 1. Strategic Goal

The goal is not to create a generic UX/UI portfolio.

The portfolio should communicate:

> This designer makes repeated product workflows easier, clearer, and less annoying — for users and the teams building them.

The portfolio should feel:

- product-led
- practical
- honest
- structured
- warm
- slightly playful
- technically aware
- easy to scan
- clear enough for recruiters
- deep enough for design leads and PMs

---

## 2. Core Positioning

### Primary Tagline

> **Easy to work with. Serious about the details.**

### Supporting Line

> I design practical digital products that make repeated workflows easier, clearer, and less annoying — for users and teams.

### Thai Direction

> คุยง่าย แต่จริงจังกับรายละเอียด

---

## 3. Core Brand Idea

> Make everyday product tasks easier, less confusing, and less annoying — for users and teams.

Key belief:

> Small frictions are not small when people face them every day.

The portfolio should show that small things like labels, states, spacing, handoff notes, and interaction patterns matter because people repeat these tasks often.

---

## 4. Professional Identity

The designer is primarily a:

> UX/UI Designer

But not a screen-only designer.

The portfolio can show interest in:

- AI-assisted design workflow
- design systems
- front-end-aware prototyping
- internal tools
- workflow-heavy products
- UX engineering-adjacent work

Do not over-position as a full UX Engineer yet.

Recommended identity line:

> UX/UI Designer focused on workflow-heavy products, internal tools, design systems, and front-end-aware prototyping.

---

## 5. Target Roles

Primary:

1. UX/UI Designer
2. Product Designer
3. UX/UI Designer for internal tools / enterprise systems
4. Product Designer for workflow-heavy products

Secondary / emerging:

1. AI-assisted design workflow
2. Design system work
3. UX Engineer-adjacent prototyping
4. Front-end-aware product design

---

## 6. Audience Types

### Recruiter

They want to know:

- Who are you?
- What role are you targeting?
- Are your projects relevant?
- Can they contact you easily?

Portfolio response:

- clear role line
- scannable homepage
- obvious Selected Work
- easy Contact / Resume access
- no vague buzzwords

### Design Lead

They want to know:

- Can you think through complexity?
- Can you explain trade-offs?
- Are you honest about scope?
- Do you make decisions with reasons?

Portfolio response:

- decision-led case studies
- clear role and scope
- before / decision / after
- constraints and trade-offs
- honest outcome / reflection

### Product Manager / Business Stakeholder

They want to know:

- Can you understand product constraints?
- Can you help teams align?
- Can you make unclear workflows easier to discuss?

Portfolio response:

- product context
- workflow problems
- stakeholder alignment
- design decisions linked to practical clarity

### Developer / Technical Collaborator

They want to know:

- Do you understand handoff?
- Do you document states and edge cases?
- Can design be implemented clearly?

Portfolio response:

- handoff notes
- component logic
- state documentation
- reusable patterns
- front-end-aware prototype or Lab when real

---

## 7. Communication Principle

The portfolio should behave like a structured conversation:

```text
Visitor question → Portfolio response → Evidence → Next action
```

Each section should answer a question in the visitor's mind.

The website should not simply say:

> Here are my works.

It should communicate:

> Here is how I think, how I reduce confusion, how I make product logic visible, and why working with me should feel clear and comfortable.

---

## 8. Desired Tone

The voice should be:

- clear
- honest
- practical
- warm
- calm
- slightly playful
- self-aware
- collaborative
- serious when it matters
- free from generic UX jargon

Avoid sounding:

- arrogant
- guru-like
- overly corporate
- overly cheerful startup
- like a design influencer
- fake-confident
- too technical-terminal
- too artsy without product proof

---

## 9. Personal Character Layer

The public brand can include a small amount of personality.

Recommended mix:

```text
Product / UX clarity: 45%
System / structure: 25%
Personality / warmth / dry humor: 15%
Motion / interaction craft: 10%
AI + code experiment: 5% on homepage, more in Lab
```

Useful phrase:

> Structured enough to trust. Human enough to remember.

Potential personal tone:

- professional but not stiff
- structured but not lifeless
- playful in conversation, serious in execution
- detail-oriented without sounding obsessive

---

## 10. What This Portfolio Should Not Become

Do not make it:

- a generic UX portfolio template
- a visual-only gallery
- a SaaS dashboard clone
- a developer portfolio
- a dark cinematic art website
- an Awwwards-style creative chaos site
- a design influencer personal brand
- an overly polished corporate consultant site
- a buzzword-heavy case study archive

---

## 11. Strategy Pass Criteria

The strategy is working if a viewer can understand within 10 seconds:

- this person is a UX/UI Designer
- they work on workflow-heavy products
- they reduce confusion for users and teams
- they care about details with reasons
- they are approachable and practical
- the portfolio has personality without losing clarity
