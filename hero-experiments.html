Concept 05 — Risks

Empty State

  • The ~2s empty moment before content resolves reads as slow load unless the blinking cursor signals intentionality. Users who don't read it as deliberate will assume something is broken.
  • Terminal-style cursor + monospace labels borrow from developer aesthetics. For a UX/UI designer, this can blur the positioning signal — "is this a developer portfolio?" Copy and frame must work harder to anchor the identity.
  • If JavaScript fails or loads slowly, the hero is literally blank. Requires a CSS no-js fallback to show content by default.
  • The bordered frame at very wide viewports (1400px+) floats in open space — needs max-width centering decisions or the frame metaphor breaks down.
  • On mobile, box-frame + monospace palette can read clinical rather than editorial. The copy carries all the warmth.

Concept 08 — Risks (updated direction)

The Audit — product-thinking framing

  • The annotation pins (01 on "product", 04 on "change it.") skip findings 02 and 03 — creates a discontinuity between pin numbering and the panel. Options: use a single pin showing total count ("04"), or add pins only to the finding rows and remove headline pins.
  • The four findings (Workflow / State / Decision / Handoff) describe a generalised pattern, not a named project. Visitors will ask: "Which product did you audit?" The CTA "See what happened next" must land on a real case study — a placeholder kills the credibility built by the hero.
  • The supporting copy sentence is doing heavy lifting at 36 words. Target 18–22 for a hero context. Suggested tighter version: "I find where workflows, states, and decisions stop making sense — then redesign them into clearer product experiences."
  • The mobile audit fragment (CSS-only ghost UI) communicates the visual language well as a placeholder but reads abstract. A real cropped screenshot — a workflow map, a whiteboard audit, or a Figma canvas fragment — would sharpen "I looked at this specific product."
  • On tablet (769px–960px), the desktop layout stacks the panel below the left column at up to 500px wide. At this width the headline is still large (clamped ~72px) — verify the visual weight balance doesn't feel top-heavy.
— Recommendation

The direction is right.
Three things to sharpen before shipping.

The copy shift works. "I read the product before I change it" is more specific, more confident, and immediately positions you as a process-first designer — not someone who makes things look good. The findings panel (Workflow / State / Decision / Handoff) gives the hero proof, not just claim. The form is already starting to demonstrate the message.

Resolve the pin-to-finding connection. Currently pins 01 and 04 appear on the headline but the panel has four rows. The gap between those numbers creates a question: "where are 02 and 03?" Either remove the numbered pins from the headline and use a single unlabelled mark, or move to four pins — one per key concept word (workflow, states, decisions, handoff) in the supporting copy rather than the headline. The visual metaphor is strong; the specific numbers need to be internally consistent.

Tighten the supporting copy. At 36 words it reads like a brief, not a hero. Target 18–22: "I find where workflows, states, and decisions stop making sense — then redesign them into clearer product experiences." Every word cut makes the finding rows land harder.

The mobile audit card is the right instinct. Headline → fragment → findings → CTA is a better sequence than stacked desktop columns. The one missing piece is a real artefact in the fragment slot — a cropped screenshot of an actual audit canvas, wireframe annotation, or Figma inspection view would make "I read the product" feel specific rather than claimed. That single image is worth more than any copy change.
